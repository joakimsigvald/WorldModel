# 1. Photon Spin From Icosahedral Geometry

## 1.1 Structure Recap
A photon in our model = **1 icosahedral shell** of **12 dissolved 4‑loops**:

- Each vertex = one 4‑loop  
- Each vertex has **5 shell bonds**  
- Shell carries a **phase** \(\phi_i(t)\) at each vertex  
- Phase synchronization across the shell determines polarization and spin

Photons propagate through the EM 4‑loop lattice.

---

## 1.2 Direction of Motion and 5‑Ring Decomposition
For a propagation direction **k**, the icosahedron naturally splits into:

- **1 front vertex** (closest to k)
- **1 back vertex** (opposite k)
- **A ring of 5 vertices** around the front
- **A ring of 5 vertices** around the back

Thus, for any direction of motion, the shell has a:

**front pole → front 5‑ring → back 5‑ring → back pole**  
structure.

---

## 1.3 Spin = Rotating Phase Pattern on the Two Rings
The key insight:

- The **phase pattern** on the *5 vertices* around the ring can rotate:
  - **clockwise**  
  - **counter‑clockwise**

These two options remain self‑consistent across:

- the 5 nodes on each ring  
- the full icosahedral connectivity  
- the couplings to the EM lattice

Thus the photon has **two global chiral phase modes**:

- **Right‑handed helicity (+1)**  
- **Left‑handed helicity (−1)**  

These correspond to physical photon spin.

---

## 1.4 Why Only Two Helicity States Exist
The icosahedral graph permits many possible phase patterns, but:

- Only **two** can propagate as stable transverse modes.
- Higher winding numbers are incompatible with the ring structure.
- The **front/back poles** act as fixed nodes that enforce coherence.

So the model reproduces the observed fact:

> **Photons have exactly two spin states.**
